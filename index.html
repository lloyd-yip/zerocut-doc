1The big idea: every video is a recipe

🧾 The recipe (we call it "the manifest")

Every video gets one master document — a recipe — that describes everything about it: what was said and exactly when, where every cut happens, which graphic appears at which moment, what music plays, which sound effects hit where. The finished video is always cooked from this recipe by a machine. No human ever hand-edits the final video. If you want something changed, you change the recipe and the machine re-cooks it in minutes — and it comes out exactly the same way every time, plus your change.

Why this matters: on the call we imagined AI "figuring out" how to assemble the video from a pile of ingredients. The problem is that a system you want to work 99 times out of 100 can't have an AI improvising at the finish line. So instead: AI writes the recipe, humans approve the recipe, and dumb reliable code cooks it. The creative judgment happens where it's cheap to review (a list of proposals you can approve on your phone), and the execution happens where it's guaranteed to be consistent (code).

The other big shift from the call: the goal isn't just editing faster — it's a system that gets better every video. Every published video sends its performance data back (where viewers dropped off, which thumbnail won), and that data tunes the scripting, the graphics choices, and the timing rules. Speed is the feature; the learning loop is the moat.

2The two spines that hold the whole thing up

This was your instinct on the call, and it's the right one: nothing here is a pile of disconnected scripts. Two tools run the entire length of the assembly line and hold everything together — one behind the scenes, one in front. Every step below plugs into both.

⚙️ n8n — the backend spine

The conductor · behind the scenes

n8n is the deterministic backbone. It kicks off every step in order, hands work to the right AI or tool at each stage, waits at the checkpoints, and moves the video down the line — the same way, every time. Think of it as the conveyor belt itself. The individual steps do clever, probabilistic things (an AI deciding where a graphic goes); n8n is the rigid, reliable frame that carries them in the right order from raw footage all the way to publish.

📋 Airtable — the frontend spine

The dashboard · what you see & touch

Airtable is the human-facing backbone. Every video is a row; every column shows what stage it's at and holds the raw ingredients to review. It's also where all your approvals happen. You never build or open a custom app — you just watch the board and click approve. This is what you meant on the call by "n8n plus a spreadsheet is basically Clay, minus the cost." (Notion works exactly the same way if you'd rather use that — pick whichever you live in.)

In the assembly line below, you'll see these two as colored rails running down both sides — n8n on the left (driving each step) and Airtable on the right (showing status & holding approvals). The steps live between them because they're literally held up by both.

3What we're building, and why

🎬 The content problem (Scott)

Videos currently ship at "40% quality" and post-production is the bottleneck. The target look is the case-study channel Scott showed on the call: short, punchy, value-packed videos with branded graphics on screen, animated text, and B-roll — the channel that went from zero to 9,500 subscribers with just 31 videos in about four months.

Scott keeps owning the scripting and the storytelling. ZeroCut owns everything that happens after he stops recording.

📈 The business logic (from the call)

Organic content feeds the paid ads engine, and organic video + AI search is the highest-leverage marketing there is — the thing that takes QS toward $1M/month. Down the road, this same system could be offered to clients as its own business.

Target: 2–4 videos a week flowing through, at 5–10× the editing speed of doing it by hand. Shorts come out of the same machine for free.

4What we kept from the call — your thinking that held up

Before the changes, credit where it's due: most of the architecture you sketched on the call survived the pressure test intact. These are the load-bearing ideas we kept exactly as you framed them.

KEPT  The conveyor-belt model

Your framing — a video moving "down the conveyor belt of each step, each node" — is literally the shape of the whole system. That's the assembly line below.

KEPT  Deterministic spine, smart nodes

Your exact point: the workflow between steps should be rigid and repeatable, while the AI does the clever, probabilistic work inside each step. That's why n8n is the spine and the models live in the nodes.

KEPT  The transcript is the master reference

You landed on the transcript being the thing everything hangs off — cuts, graphics, timing all reference it. That became the recipe. Strongest idea in the call.

KEPT  Human gates at the right spots

You co-designed roughly five human checkpoints — transcript, cutting, animation ideas, final. We kept the checkpoints; we just made most of them fall away over time as trust builds.

KEPT  Rent the frontend, don't build one

Your call: no custom app — a spreadsheet frontend (Airtable/Notion) is more than enough, and pairing it with n8n gives you Clay without the Clay bill. Kept as the frontend spine.

KEPT  Understand it before automating it

Your principle — you should know how to do the job by hand before you automate it, or it turns to slop. Scott's already done the manual steps, so we're clear to build.

KEPT  Always adversarially test the AI

Your point that AI feels like an expert on everything except what you actually know well — so its first draft is usually flawed, and something (a human or another agent) has to catch it. That's baked into every checkpoint.

KEPT  Organic feeds paid; build it AI-first

The whole strategic frame — organic assets get recycled into paid, so build the AI-native version now rather than hiring a human to do it manually. Unchanged.

5What changed since the call — and why

Four things changed in important ways once the plan got stress-tested:

On the call we said…What we're actually doing, and why
"Adobe Premiere's MCP will assemble the video" — the AI drives Premiere to plug all the ingredients in. CHANGED Those Premiere remote-controls do exist — but they're hobby-grade, need Premiere open on a desktop, and they're an AI puppeting a fragile app. Fine for a human sitting there; wrong for a machine that must work unattended every time. Instead, a proper video-rendering tool (Remotion) cooks the video directly from the recipe. Premiere stays around only as a break-glass option for the rare video that needs real hand-craft.
"The Mac Studio runs a video AI (LTX) to make the animations." MOSTLY KILLED The thing we got wrong: look closely at the channel we admire — those aren't AI-generated videos, they're motion graphics: branded charts, animated text, clean diagrams. AI video generators make dreamy realistic footage; they cannot make crisp on-brand graphics with readable text, and they never produce the same thing twice. The right tool is a small library of reusable branded templates — designed once, then filled in automatically per video. Same look every time, instant, free. The Mac Studio still earns its keep doing the rendering and the occasional genuinely-AI B-roll shot.
"We'll build the transcript-cutting step ourselves." BUY INSTEAD Cutting out silences, "ums," and bad takes from a transcript is a solved problem — a $20/month tool called Gling does exactly this today. Building it would burn two weeks recreating something we can rent. The custom stuff worth building is what nobody sells: the design brain, the brand templates, and the learning loop.
"AI reads the transcript and decides where the animations go." UPGRADED A transcript tells you what was said — not how. Great editing syncs graphics to the delivery: the pause before the punchline, the emphasis on a number, the beat of the music. So the system now also listens to the audio itself (where the pauses are, where the voice punches, where the music beats fall) and a simple timing rule nudges every graphic onto those natural moments. The AI decides what goes where; the audio decides exactly when. Without this, every graphic lands technically-correct but half a second emotionally off.
"Maybe you don't need a human in the loop at the end." REVERSED Flipped it: the early checkpoints are training wheels that come off as the machine proves itself — but the final watch-through never goes away. A human always watches the finished video before it publishes. Everything else earns trust; that one is forever.
(Not discussed on the call) — what happens after publishing. ADDED The biggest addition. YouTube hands us, for free, the exact second viewers leave every video and which thumbnail wins an A/B test. That data now flows back into the system: retention dips tune the scripting, thumbnail results tune the thumbnails, timing complaints tune the timing rules. This loop is the thing competitors can't copy — anyone can automate editing; nobody else has your accumulating data about what your audience actually watches.

MACHINE

Step 0 · Drop the footage in — and n8n takes over

What happens: Scott finishes recording and drags the raw video plus his script into a folder. That's his whole job here — about 2 minutes. From this instant, n8n is in control: it notices the new file, creates a fresh recipe for this video, adds a row to the Airtable board, and starts walking the video through every step that follows — waiting at each checkpoint for a human, then carrying on. It is the spine that connects Step 0 all the way to publish.

⚙️ n8n starts the run📋 Airtable row created
n8n — the conductor of the whole assembly line. A free, self-hosted automation tool (running on Scott's Mac Studio) that watches for work, kicks off each step in order, and waits at checkpoints. Think Zapier, but self-hosted and free — and here it runs the entire pipeline, not just Step 0. Airtable — the tracking board. A spreadsheet-on-steroids where every row is a video and the columns show what stage it's at. Also where all approvals happen. (Notion works identically.)
▼
MACHINE

Step 1 · Listen to the recording

What happens: the machine transcribes the video word by word, noting the exact moment each word is spoken. Then it listens a second time for the performance: where Scott pauses, where his voice gets louder, which words he punches. All of it goes into the recipe.

The transcript alone tells us what was said. The pauses and emphasis tell us how it was said — and that's what everything downstream syncs to. This is what makes the graphics later land on the natural rhythm of the delivery instead of feeling pasted on.
⚙️ n8n runs this step📋 status → "transcribed"
WhisperX — free speech-to-text that runs on the Mac (nothing leaves the machine). Its specialty: it timestamps every single word, the backbone of the whole recipe. librosa — a free audio-analysis library. It doesn't understand words; it measures sound — where the silences are, where the volume spikes. Cheap, instant, same answer every time.
▼
MACHINE

Step 2 · The rough cut

What happens: all the "ums," long silences, false starts, and repeated takes get stripped out automatically, leaving a clean version of the talk. The machine then renders a quick low-quality preview — just good enough to check the cuts.

This is the most tedious part of manual editing — and it's a solved problem we can rent for $20/month instead of building. Getting the retakes out first also means every later step works from the clean version of what was actually said.
⚙️ n8n sends footage to Gling, gets timeline back📋 preview attached to the row
Gling — a service built for exactly this: feed it raw talking-head footage, it removes silences, fillers, and bad takes. $20/month. FFmpeg — the free Swiss-army knife of video. Here it just stitches a fast preview so a human can check the cut without waiting for a full render.
▼
🧍
Checkpoint A — "Did it cut the right things?"

Who: Scott (or the editor). Where: in Airtable. Time: 5–10 minutes. Skim the preview and flag anything wrong: "restore this bit," "trim that." Fixes go into the recipe and a new preview appears in minutes. Passing this also confirms the transcript is right, so there's no separate transcript check.

⏳ Training wheels: once 10 videos in a row sail through nearly untouched, this checkpoint switches off.
▼
AI SUGGESTS

Step 3 · The design brain proposes the edit

What happens: Claude reads the clean transcript — including the pause and emphasis markers from Step 1 — plus the catalog of brand templates and the sound-effects library. It then writes a proposal list: "put the revenue-counter graphic on the phrase 'two million dollars'… show a checklist graphic while he lists the three steps… B-roll here… this whoosh sound there… use this music track… and these two segments would make great shorts." Each suggestion says which template, with what text, anchored to which words.

This is the creative judgment step — the one thing that genuinely needs AI. But notice what it produces: a list of suggestions, not a video. A list is cheap to review, edit, and reject line by line. A rendered video is expensive to redo. The AI proposes; it doesn't decide. And it deliberately does NOT set exact timing — that comes next, and it's not an AI job.
⚙️ n8n calls Claude, writes proposals to the recipe📋 proposals appear as rows to approve
Claude — reads the annotated transcript and proposes the creative plan. Costs roughly $1–3 per video. Epidemic Sound (or similar) — a music and sound-effects subscription (~$15–30/mo). Sounds are always picked from this library, never AI-generated — picking from a good library is more reliable than generating, every time.
▼
🧍
Checkpoint B — "Do I like the plan?"

Who: Scott. Where: in Airtable (works fine on a phone). Time: 10–15 minutes. Every suggestion is a row: approve it, tweak the wording, or reject it. Music choice included. This is the highest-leverage 15 minutes in the whole process — you're steering the creative direction of the video before a single expensive thing gets made.

⏳ Training wheels: once the AI is getting >90% of proposals approved, this drops to spot-checks.
▼
PLAIN CODE

Step 3½ · Snap everything to the rhythm

What happens: a simple timing rule goes through every approved suggestion and nudges it onto the nearest natural moment: text graphics land in the breath after the key word (never mid-word), sound effects hit exactly on a music beat, B-roll cuts on pauses or downbeats, and reveals land on the dramatic pause before the payoff line.

This is what separates an edit that feels alive from one that feels off. A graphic that appears while Scott is still mid-word feels wrong even if nobody can say why. And crucially, this is not an AI decision — it's a measurable rule ("move to the nearest beat") that behaves identically every time. If the timing ever feels off across several videos, we adjust the rule once and every future video inherits the fix.
⚙️ n8n runs the snap, no human needed
Plain code — a lookup, not a model. The timing rules live in one config file we tune over time.
▼
MACHINE

Step 4 · Make the graphics

What happens: the approved suggestions get turned into actual visual assets. About 90% come from the brand template library — pre-designed animated graphics (a stat counter, a title card, a checklist, a quote card, a chart) that get filled in with this video's text and numbers. Identical brand look every time, generated instantly. The rare shot that genuinely needs AI-generated footage gets queued on the Mac Studio.

This is the correction of the biggest wrong turn from the call. The channel we're modeling doesn't use AI-generated video — it uses a consistent set of branded motion graphics. Templates give us exactly that: designed once by a pro, reused forever, always on-brand, always legible. The template library is arguably the most valuable single asset in this whole system — it literally is the channel's visual identity.
⚙️ n8n renders templates, queues any AI shots📋 assets attach to the row
Remotion — free tool where animated graphics are defined like code templates with blanks to fill ("headline goes here, number goes there"). Fill the blanks, get the finished animation. After Effects — where a motion designer authors the master templates in the first place (one-time work). LTX — a free AI video generator running locally on the Mac Studio, for the occasional genuinely-generative B-roll shot. A bit part, not the star.
▼
🧍
Checkpoint C — "Do the AI shots look right?"

Who: Scott. Where: in Airtable. Time: ~2 minutes, only when AI-generated shots exist. They show up as a grid of thumbnails: approve, regenerate, or swap for a template. Template graphics skip this entirely — they can't surprise you, so there's nothing to check.

⏳ Only fires on videos that actually use generative shots.
▼
PLAIN CODE

Step 5 · Cook the video

What happens: the machine takes the completed recipe — footage, cuts, graphics, music, sound effects, all with exact timings — and renders the finished video. The same recipe always produces the same video. Shorts get cooked from the same recipe: the approved highlight segments are re-framed vertical and rendered alongside.

Because this is pure execution of an approved recipe — zero judgment involved — it needs no checkpoint and can never "go rogue." This step is also why nobody edits timelines anymore: the video is always re-creatable from the recipe in minutes, so changes are safe, repeatable, and never lost.
⚙️ n8n triggers the render on the Mac Studio📋 status → "rendered"
Remotion — the one and only renderer. Runs on the Mac Studio.
▼
🧍
Checkpoint D — the final watch-through PERMANENT

Who: the editor — in his new role. Where: in Airtable. He watches the finished video like a viewer would and leaves timestamped notes: "cut at 3:12 feels early," "that overlay covers his face," "sound effect too loud." Each note updates the recipe, the machine re-cooks in minutes, he re-checks. When it's right, he approves — and the recipe locks.

The compounding rule: if the same kind of note keeps coming up across videos, that's not a video problem — it's a rule or template problem. We fix the template or the timing rule once, and every future video comes out already fixed. This is how the machine literally gets better every week.

This checkpoint never switches off. Every other checkpoint is training wheels; a human watching the final video before the world does is forever.

⏏ The escape hatch (rare, one-way)

Occasionally a video will need real hand-craft the recipe can't express. For those: the system exports a normal Premiere project once, the editor finishes it by hand, and that video permanently leaves the assembly line — its recipe is frozen and never re-cooked (otherwise the machine would overwrite his handiwork). The health metric: if more than 1 in 5 videos needs the escape hatch, the template library is too weak — we fix the library, not the videos.

▼
MACHINE

Step 6 · Publish — and learn

What happens: the video and its shorts upload to YouTube automatically (Scott just confirms title and description — 2 minutes). Up to three thumbnail versions go into YouTube's built-in A/B test. Then, every night, the system pulls the numbers back: the exact retention curve (the second-by-second graph of when viewers leave) and which thumbnail is winning.

This is the step that was completely missing from the original plan, and it's the whole ballgame. The retention data gets lined up against the recipe — "viewers bailed at 3:12, which was a 40-second stretch with no B-roll" — and those findings feed back into Scott's scripting, the design brain's choices, and the timing rules. Every video published makes the next one better. Anyone can copy an automation; nobody can copy months of your audience's behavior data wired into your machine.
⚙️ n8n uploads + pulls analytics nightly📋 performance saved back to the row
YouTube Data API + Analytics API — free. One uploads and runs thumbnail A/B tests; the other hands back retention and click-through data. Pixels.app — the thumbnail generator Scott already uses. Stays manual for now (his call, and the right one — it barely takes time). The only new wiring: A/B results get saved so the thumbnail style choices become data-driven. Supabase — the free database where every recipe and every performance number lives. It's the system's memory behind Airtable.

…and back to the top. The learning from Step 6 flows into the next video's script, design proposals, and timing rules. The assembly line isn't just fast — it's compounding.

9The build plan — about a month, in the right order

The counterintuitive rule: build the cooking before the thinking. If the renderer can't produce a video that looks good, no amount of AI on top matters — so we prove that first, with a recipe written by hand. And through all of it: publishing never pauses for the build.

WEEK 1

Prove the kitchen. Hand-write a recipe for a video that's already published, have Remotion cook it, and compare it side-by-side with the human-edited original. Also: run one real video through Gling ($20 experiment). If the render isn't within striking distance of the human edit, we stop and fix templates before building anything else. Also lock the recipe format — everything depends on it.

WEEK 2

Build the look. The 5 core brand templates, professionally designed, with their timing rules. Plus the listening step (transcription + audio analysis).

WEEK 3

Build the brain. The design pass, the rhythm-snapping rules (tested carefully — this is where subtle timing bugs would hide), the Airtable checkpoints, and timers on every checkpoint so "a video is stuck waiting on a human" shows up as a number, not a surprise.

WEEK 4

Connect it end-to-end. Full pipeline through publish, with n8n wired as the spine across all steps. The first 10 videos are calibration runs — expect lots of Checkpoint D notes; that's the system being tuned, not failing.

AFTER

Wire the learning loop. Retention and thumbnail data flowing back automatically. Thumbnail automation waits until the data shows which styles actually win clicks.

10Decisions still open (human calls, not tech ones)

  1. The editor conversation. His job changes from "edits videos" to "reviews videos and improves templates." The system only works if he genuinely never opens Premiere on normal videos. Scott needs to have this conversation directly — it's the single biggest human risk in the plan.
  2. Airtable or Notion? Either works as the frontend spine — pick whichever you already live in. (Doc currently assumes Airtable.)
  3. Pick the music library. Beat-synced editing needs a known, licensed music track per video (Epidemic Sound or similar). Videos without music still work — graphics sync to pauses instead of beats.
  4. Hire the template designer. One-time engagement, five templates, professional standard. These templates are the channel's visual identity — worth real money done right.
